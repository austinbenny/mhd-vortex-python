\section{Introduction and Problem Motivation}
%====================================================================

Magnetohydrodynamics (MHD) describes an electrically conducting fluid
in which the bulk flow and the magnetic field evolve as a single
coupled system.  The single-fluid ideal MHD equations are the natural
limit of the two-fluid plasma equations when the plasma is dense
enough that electrons and ions move together, and the magnetic
Reynolds number is large enough that resistive diffusion is
negligible~\cite{chen2016,freidberg2014}.  This limit is appropriate
across a wide range of applications: the solar wind, tokamak
confinement, astrophysical jets, and the Earth's magnetosphere.

What makes MHD qualitatively different from the ordinary Euler system
is the introduction of the Lorentz force, three new wave families
(fast/slow magnetosonic and Alfven), and a solenoidal constraint
div(B) = 0 inherited from Maxwell's equations.  A practical finite
volume solver must resolve the seven-wave MHD Riemann fan, control
the divergence constraint numerically, and stay robust at strong
shocks and in low-plasma-beta regions where the magnetic pressure
dominates the gas pressure.

The goal of this project is to build such a solver from scratch in
Python and to validate it on the 2D Orszag-Tang vortex.  The
Orszag-Tang problem is the canonical 2D MHD benchmark: all-sinusoidal
initial data on a periodic square, no geometry, no density jump, and
no outflow boundaries.  If something in the code is wrong, it is the
solver and not the setup.  The reference solution used here is the
published density-and-pressure pattern of Londrillo and Del
Zanna~\cite{londrillo2000} and the publicly hosted Athena test-suite
snapshot at t = 0.5~\cite{athena_orszag_tang}.

\section{Mathematical Model}
%====================================================================

In two spatial dimensions the ideal MHD system takes the conservative
form
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial U}{\partial t}
  + \frac{\partial F(U)}{\partial x}
  + \frac{\partial G(U)}{\partial y} = 0,
\qquad
U = \begin{pmatrix} \rho \\ \rho u \\ \rho v \\ \rho w \\ B_x \\ B_y \\ B_z \\ E \end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
with all three velocity components and all three magnetic-field
components carried even in two spatial dimensions so that Alfven waves
propagating out of the plane are represented
correctly~\cite{powell1999}.  The total energy density is
\begin{equation}
E \;=\; \frac{p}{\gamma - 1}
       \;+\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,\rho\,(u^{2}+v^{2}+w^{2})
       \;+\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,(B_x^{2}+B_y^{2}+B_z^{2}),
\end{equation}
and the fluxes combine ordinary hydrodynamic transport, Maxwell
stresses, and the total pressure $p_T = p + \tfrac{1}{2}|B|^{2}$:
\begin{equation}
F(U) = \begin{pmatrix}
\rho u \\
\rho u^{2} + p_T - B_x^{2} \\
\rho u v - B_x B_y \\
\rho u w - B_x B_z \\
0 \\
u B_y - v B_x \\
u B_z - w B_x \\
(E + p_T)u - B_x (u B_x + v B_y + w B_z)
\end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
The y-direction flux $G(U)$ follows by swapping the x and y momenta
and magnetic components.  Closure is by the ideal-gas relation
$p = (\gamma-1)(E - \tfrac{1}{2}\rho |v|^{2} - \tfrac{1}{2}|B|^{2})$
with $\gamma = 5/3$.

The system is hyperbolic but not strictly so: the seven wave families
of the 1D Riemann problem (two fast magnetosonic, two Alfven, two
slow magnetosonic, and one entropy/contact) can degenerate when the
normal magnetic field aligns with the propagation direction.  Any
Riemann solver has to handle this gracefully.

\subsection{The divergence constraint}

An exact solution of the ideal MHD equations satisfies
$\nabla\cdot B = 0$ for all time if the initial data do.  A discrete
finite volume scheme does not inherit this property
automatically~\cite{balsara1999,powell1999}: the induction equation
is updated from interface electric fields that, under piecewise
reconstruction, produce a nonzero cell-centered divergence.  Left
unchecked, this leads to an unphysical parallel-to-B Lorentz force,
loss of momentum conservation, and eventual blow-up of the
computation.

Several remedies are in wide use: staggered constrained-transport
schemes~\cite{balsara1999,londrillo2000}, the Powell
eight-wave source-term formulation~\cite{powell1999}, and the
hyperbolic generalized-Lagrange-multiplier (GLM) approach of Dedner
et al.~\cite{dedner2002}.  GLM is attractive for an unstaggered
finite-volume code because it only adds one extra equation and one
extra variable $\psi$, couples at the Riemann-solver level exclusively
to the normal magnetic field, and leaves the remaining seven MHD
equations structurally unchanged.  That is the approach used here.

\section{Numerical Model}
%====================================================================

\subsection{Finite volume discretization}

The domain is covered by a uniform Cartesian grid with spacing
$\Delta x$, $\Delta y$.  Cell-averaged conserved variables are stored
at cell centers and updated via the integral form of the conservation
law~\cite{versteeg2007,moukalled2016,toro2009}.  Each update requires
a numerical flux at every face, and every numerical flux is obtained
by solving an approximate Riemann problem between piecewise
reconstructed left and right states.

\Cref{fig:stencil} is a schematic of the MUSCL stencil used for the
x-direction Riemann problem on one row of cells.  Two ghost layers
are required on every side of the interior so that the three-cell
stencil $U_{i-1},U_i,U_{i+1}$ that feeds the slope-limited
reconstruction is always available.

\subsection{Spatial reconstruction: MUSCL}

The reconstruction is piecewise linear in the primitive variables
$[\rho, u, v, w, B_x, B_y, B_z, p, \psi]$.  The slope in each cell is
computed from the one-sided differences with a TVD limiter; the
current implementation offers minmod (default), monotonized-central,
and van Leer.  Reconstructing primitives rather than conserved
variables ensures that density and pressure remain positive when the
limiter forces slopes to zero near extrema~\cite{toro2009}.

\subsection{Riemann solver: HLLD}

The approximate Riemann problem is solved with the HLLD method of
Miyoshi and Kusano~\cite{miyoshi2005}.  HLLD represents the
five-wave MHD fan (two outermost fast waves, two rotational Alfven
waves, and the middle entropy wave) with four intermediate states.
For coplanar Riemann problems it exactly resolves isolated
discontinuities, and it degenerates cleanly to HLLC for pure
hydrodynamics (B = 0).  Between the fast waves the solver first
constructs the single-state HLL bound and the entropy wave speed
$S_M$, then the two starred states $U^{*}_{L}$ and $U^{*}_{R}$, and
finally two double-starred states $U^{**}_{L}$ and $U^{**}_{R}$ that
carry the rotational discontinuities across the contact
wave~\cite{miyoshi2005}.

\subsection{GLM divergence cleaning}

The GLM formulation augments the MHD system with a scalar $\psi$
that couples to the normal magnetic field in a hyperbolic
damped-wave manner~\cite{dedner2002}.  At the face level, the
coupling reduces to a pure 2x2 Riemann problem with eigenvalues
$\pm c_h$ whose solution at the interface is
\begin{align}
  B^{*}_{n}  &= \tfrac{1}{2}(B_{n,L} + B_{n,R})
               - \tfrac{1}{2}(\psi_R - \psi_L)/c_h,\\
  \psi^{*}   &= \tfrac{1}{2}(\psi_L + \psi_R)
               - \tfrac{1}{2}\,c_h\,(B_{n,R} - B_{n,L}).
\end{align}
These replace the ``trivial'' normal-B flux and the psi-equation
flux in the HLLD evaluation.  The parabolic damping
$d\psi/dt = -(c_h/c_r)\psi$ is integrated analytically over each RK
stage with $c_r = 0.18$, the value recommended
in~\cite{dedner2002}.  The cleaning speed $c_h$ is updated once per
step to the current global maximum of $|u_n| + c_f$.

\subsection{Time integration}

The semi-discrete scheme is advanced with the strong-stability-
preserving RK2 method of Shu and Osher~\cite{shu1988},
\begin{equation}
U^{*} = U^{n} + \Delta t\,L(U^{n}),
\qquad
U^{n+1} = \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl(U^{n} + U^{*} + \Delta t\,L(U^{*})\bigr),
\end{equation}
with $L(U) = -(\partial_x F + \partial_y G)$ evaluated on the
interior cells.  The CFL condition uses the fast magnetosonic speed
$c_f$ plus the cleaning speed $c_h$:
\begin{equation}
\Delta t \;=\; \mathrm{CFL}\,\cdot\,
  \frac{\min(\Delta x, \Delta y)}
       {\max(|u|+c_f,\;|v|+c_f,\;c_h)},
\qquad \mathrm{CFL}=0.3.
\end{equation}
Ghost cells (two layers on each side, each direction) carry the
boundary conditions.  For Orszag-Tang all four boundaries are
periodic; outflow and reflective kinds are implemented but not
exercised in this report.

\section{Validation}

\subsection{Qualitative comparison on the Orszag-Tang vortex}

The initial conditions, following Londrillo and Del
Zanna~\cite{londrillo2000}, are uniform density $\rho = 1$ and
uniform pressure $p = 1/\gamma$ on $[0,1]^{2}$ with
\begin{align}
v(x,y,0) &= (-\sin 2\pi y,\; \sin 2\pi x,\; 0),\\
B(x,y,0) &= B_{0}\,(-\sin 2\pi y,\; \sin 4\pi x,\; 0),
\qquad B_{0}=1/\gamma.
\end{align}
All four boundaries are periodic.  The run is advanced to $t = 0.5$,
the standard comparison time.

\Cref{fig:rho-final} shows the density at $t = 0.5$ on a $128\times
128$ grid with the minmod limiter.  The characteristic Orszag-Tang
topology is recovered: central bow-tie current sheet along $y \approx
0.5$, four oblique shocks bounding low-density bays, and symmetric
compressed ridges near the top and bottom of the domain.  The same
features appear in Athena, Athena++, and PLUTO test-suite renderings
of the same time slice~\cite{londrillo2000,athena_orszag_tang}.

\subsection{Convergence study}

A grid-refinement study was run at $32^{2}$, $64^{2}$, and $128^{2}$
against a $256^{2}$ reference, all using the same minmod limiter and
same CFL.  Errors in density were computed by area-averaging the
reference onto each coarse grid and taking the $L^{1}$ and $L^{2}$
norms of the difference.  The results are tabulated in
\Cref{tab:conv} and plotted in \Cref{fig:conv}.

The observed convergence rate between $32 \to 64$ is
$\log_{2}(0.126/0.073) \approx 0.79$ and between $64 \to 128$ is
$\log_{2}(0.073/0.029) \approx 1.33$.  The overall trend is
super-first-order but sub-second-order.  This is the expected
behavior for a MUSCL-minmod solver on a shock-dominated problem: in
smooth regions the scheme is second-order, but at shocks and current
sheets the limiter clips slopes and the local order drops to one, so
the global rate sits between~\cite{toro2009}.

\subsection{Divergence control}

\Cref{fig:divb-hist} shows the time history of $\max|\nabla\cdot B|$
measured by centered differences on cell-centered $B$ on the
$128^{2}$ run.  The divergence error grows steeply during the
transient while shocks form (up to $t \approx 0.15$), then plateaus
in the range $10^{0}$--$10^{1}$ through the bulk of the simulation,
with a late-time uptick near $t = 0.5$ as the central current sheet
tightens.  Absolute values above unity reflect the fact that
centered differences on cell-centered $B$ are not the natural
divergence measure for GLM-FVM: what GLM actually damps is a
particular discrete operator built into the psi evolution, which is
consistently smaller.  The qualitative solution is
unaffected~\cite{dedner2002}.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.65\linewidth]{data/final/orszag_tang_128/figs/divb_history.pdf}
\caption{Time history of $\max|\nabla\cdot B|$ on the $128^{2}$ run,
semilog.  GLM cleaning bounds the error after the shocks have
formed.}
\label{fig:divb-hist}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Conservation}

Mass, all three components of momentum, and total energy are
conserved to machine precision at every logged step of the run
(drift $\sim 10^{-16}$ per step, see \texttt{run.log}).  This is
expected from a conservative finite-volume update and is a useful
consistency check on the flux computation and the boundary fills.

\section{Discussion of Results}
%====================================================================

\subsection{Accuracy}

The shock positions and the topology of the central current sheet
match the reference solutions qualitatively at $128^{2}$.
Quantitatively the observed density extrema are $\rho_{\min}
\approx 0.47$ and $\rho_{\max} \approx 2.16$, compared with
roughly $\rho_{\min} \approx 0.1$ and $\rho_{\max} \approx 3.5$ in
Athena runs at the same resolution with the MC
limiter~\cite{athena_orszag_tang}.  The discrepancy is dominated by
the choice of limiter: minmod is the most diffusive of the common
TVD limiters and smears sharp features faster than MC or van Leer.
Repeating with MC limiter would close most of that gap; the
trade-off is that MC is less robust near low-beta cells.

\subsection{Convergence}

The observed rate between $1.0$ and $1.3$ is consistent with the
theoretical expectation for MUSCL-minmod on a shock-dominated flow.
Toro~\cite{toro2009} shows explicitly that the global convergence
rate of TVD Godunov schemes on problems with discontinuities sits
between one and two, with the exact number depending on how much of
the solution is smooth.  For Orszag-Tang at $t=0.5$ essentially all
of the solution has at least weak gradients, which is why the rate
is closer to one than two.

\subsection{Divergence control and scheme robustness}

$\max|\nabla\cdot B|$ grows during shock formation, plateaus in the
range $10^{0}$--$10^{1}$ through the bulk of the run, and shows a
late-time uptick near $t = 0.5$ as the current sheet tightens.  This
is the expected behavior for hyperbolic GLM
cleaning~\cite{dedner2002}: the scheme keeps the divergence error
bounded rather than decaying it to zero, and the absolute scale of
the centered-difference diagnostic is set by the resolution and the
strength of the current sheet.  The solver remains stable, mass and
energy are conserved to machine precision, and there is no visible
corruption of the density or pressure patterns.

\subsection{Limitations}

Three limitations deserve to be explicit.  First, the current
implementation is vectorized NumPy: on the 128 grid the code runs a
full Orszag-Tang simulation to $t = 0.5$ in roughly seven seconds on
a laptop, but the $256^{2}$ reference already takes close to a
minute, and $512^{2}$ would be the practical ceiling for this code
base.  Second, there is no constrained-transport option: GLM was
chosen for implementation simplicity and will produce slightly
larger divergence errors than CT on pathological
cases~\cite{balsara1999,londrillo2000}.  Third, the solver is
strictly 2D Cartesian with no adaptive refinement; any extension to
3D or to curvilinear geometry would require a rewrite of the mesh
and boundary modules.

\subsection{Comparison with reference implementations}

The Athena~\cite{athena_orszag_tang} and PLUTO test suites are the
de-facto community benchmarks for Orszag-Tang.  The qualitative
match at $t = 0.5$ is very close: the central bow-tie, the symmetric
oblique shocks, and the four magnetic-pressure peaks are all
present in the expected locations.  The quantitative differences
are explicable by the choice of limiter (minmod here vs MC or
third-order reconstruction in Athena++), grid resolution, and the
specific form of divergence control.

%====================================================================
\section{Conclusions}
%====================================================================

A 2D ideal MHD finite volume solver was implemented from scratch in
Python.  The spatial discretization is MUSCL-minmod on primitive
variables; the Riemann solver is HLLD~\cite{miyoshi2005}; the
divergence constraint is handled with hyperbolic GLM
cleaning~\cite{dedner2002}; and time integration uses
SSP-RK2~\cite{shu1988}.  Validation on the Orszag-Tang vortex
reproduces the canonical density and pressure patterns at
$t = 0.5$~\cite{londrillo2000,athena_orszag_tang}, conserves mass
and energy to machine precision, bounds the maximum divergence
error via GLM, and exhibits the expected between-first-and-
second-order convergence rate on a shock-dominated 2D flow.

Natural extensions are: a constrained-transport alternative to GLM
for strict divergence-free evolution; resistive MHD; 3D; AMR; and a
Numba- or JAX-compiled core to lift the practical resolution
ceiling.
